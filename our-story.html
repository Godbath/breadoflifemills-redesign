Our Story

Bread of Life Mills is a reflection of our lifestyle and traditions, shaped by sustainable living, homesteading, and a desire to be more connected to the food we consume. At the core of our work is a deep respect for ancestral ways, traditional food, and the wisdom passed down through generations.

We source local Arizona wheat, mill it fresh, craft our own blends, and bake with it the same day. Our work extends far beyond baking. We function as a milling company and a resource for whole wheat berries and fresh-milled flour. The bread we bake is how we share this way of life, inspiring others to mill, bake, and make eating real bread part of their daily life. We offer education and hands-on classes, along with thoughtfully crafted wheat berry blends and mills, teaching and equipping every family to mill at home and return to tradition in their own kitchens.

We are shaped by our predominantly shared Italian heritage and old-country traditions of wholesome cooking, shared meals, and working closely with the land. Michelle’s family was rooted in New York City, living in close-knit Italian communities where food was shared, traditions were preserved, and generations contributed to the rich fabric of the city’s Italian culinary culture. Eric’s family, after immigrating to America, moved upstate where they farmed and carried on a legacy of large-scale agriculture and wine production. These traditions continue to influence our work today.

We discovered the value of using the whole wheat berry in freshly milled flour instead of modern alternatives, and it changed everything. Through that process, we began to see the impact of the American food system, built for shelf life and efficiency, where modern milling separates the grain and strips away many of its most valuable components. In doing so, the bran and germ, rich in fiber, vitamins, minerals, and essential fats, are stripped away, taking with them much of what made bread nourishing and supportive to the body. This shift has contributed to widespread issues in digestion and metabolic health, and is part of the growing chronic disease landscape we see today.

Bread, once a nourishing staple, is now something many struggle with. Our hope is to help restore traditional bread to the American home, and to bring clarity to the role modern processing has played in changing how bread is experienced today. Nothing brings us more joy than seeing people enjoy bread again and realize that gluten is not the problem.
